Economic Overview


On the whole, the United States of America (USA) boast the highest GDP per Capita, whereas Africa consistently reports the lowest figures. The USA consistently maintains the world's top GDP per Capita, underscoring the fact that its residents enjoy access to a relatively substantial economic output and income, which serves as a significant indicator of a high standard of living. Nevertheless, there exists a

substantial disparity between the highest and lowest GDP per Capita . For instance, in 2023, Sierra Leone's GDP per Capita stands at only approximately 415 million, while the USA has surged to around 80,000 million in GDP per Capita. Nonetheless, it's noteworthy that GDP per Capita figures worldwide are generally exhibiting an upward trajectory , which is a positive development.


The stacked bar chart displays the GDP composition of the top 10 countries, with the United States leading by a substantial margin, exceeding 2,200 trillion USD. China, ranked second, trails significantly with a total of 1,700 trillion USD. Beyond the top two, the disparity in economic output widens, with Japan's GDP totaling just 500 trillion USD, more than four times lower than the US and over three times lower than China.

The GDP composition in these countries is divided into agriculture, industry, and services. Across all nations, services contribute the most to their economic output. Notably, China has the least service sector contribution at around 50%, while the other countries generally have service sectors accounting for at least 70% of their economic output.



Factor affecting Economic


The heatmaps reveal the investing patterns of 12 randomly chosen countries across various sectors. Despite differences in their economic development levels, these countries share a strong emphasis on investing in Public Services, Health, Education, and Economic Affairs. This suggests that different countries had agreed on the investment fields will be better for people and economies.

Over time, there is a noticeable upward trend in healthcare spending in many of these countries. This trend is motivated by the understanding that good health is essential for a nation's prosperity. A country's economic growth is hindered when its population's health is in decline, as it can lead to increased disease prevalence, higher mortality rates, and a reduced labor force, ultimately impacting the overall economy.

The chart depicts annual changes in the unemployment rate, showcasing a generally positive trend of decreasing unemployment, which is favorable for the economy. However, a significant and unexpected spike occurred in 2020 due to the COVID-19 pandemic, leading to an unprecedented increase in unemployment. Many companies were compelled to lay off employees to mitigate losses before implementing countermeasures. Post-2020, the unemployment rate returned to more typical fluctuations.

Zooming in on Africa reveals a starkly increasing trend in unemployment, suggesting a persistent decline in the labor force. A higher unemployment rate negatively impacts GDP because it represents a waste of potential labor resources and human

capital, leading to decreased production and economic output. Unemployed individuals contribute less to consumer spending, which is a key driver of economic growth. Additionally, it can strain social safety nets and government resources, further affecting GDP through increased public expenditure.

In contrast, Europe has been successful in managing its unemployment rate, experiencing the most significant decrease among continents. The unemployment rate in Europe declined by approximately 5%, while the global average decrease was only 2%. Even during the COVID-19 pandemic, Europe was the least affected in terms of unemployment rate, indicating a stable economy and effective national planning.

Effect of Economic


The scatter plot illustrates the correlation between a country's GDP per Capita, Life Expectancy, and its Population. The data clearly indicates that as a country's GDP per Capita increases, so does its Life Expectancy. When we group countries by continent, it's evident that Africa stands out with the lowest Life Expectancy and GDP per Capita, likely due to the persistent impact of diseases leading to higher mortality rates and shorter life expectancies. This, in turn, hampers their economic development due to a lack of a productive workforce.

In contrast, Asian countries have shown the most pronounced growth in Life Expectancy and GDP per Capita. In 1995, many

Asian nations had only marginally better statistics than Africa. However, over time, their GDP per Capita has risen to levels comparable to Europe and the United States. This growth was driven by rapid industrialization, often through exports, and changes in economic structures, supported by flexible economic policies when needed.

Since 1995, the data points have generally shifted from the bottom left corner to the upper right corner, indicating improvements in both GDP per Capita and Life Expectancy. Notably, there isn't a strong correlation between population and either Life Expectancy or GDP per Capita, as different population sizes are spread across various data ranges.

Overall, life expectancy has been on the rise over the years, with Africa consistently having the lowest life expectancy among continents. While African life expectancy remains below 65 years, there has been a notable and rapid increase from 55 in 2000 to 63 in 2015, likely attributed to improved healthcare services addressing long-standing issues.

In contrast, other regions exhibit relatively modest and gradual increases in life expectancy, typically ranging from 2 to 5 years. This suggests a more steady but slower pace of improvement, possibly indicating that these areas have reached a relatively stable stage of development, encompassing comprehensive economic, healthcare, and demographic changes.
